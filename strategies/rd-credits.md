# R&D Tax Credits

## Overview

The Research and Development (R&D) Tax Credit provides dollar-for-dollar reduction of federal income tax liability for qualified research activities. This powerful credit is available to businesses of all sizes that develop new or improved products, processes, software, or techniques—not just traditional "lab coat" research.

## Key Benefits

- Dollar-for-dollar tax reduction (not just deduction)
- Up to $500,000 annual offset against payroll taxes (startups)
- Credit carryforward for 20 years
- State credits may also be available
- Available for wide range of industries
- Applicable to existing product improvements

## Credit Calculation Methods

### Regular Research Credit (RRC)
- 20% of Qualified Research Expenses (QREs) exceeding base amount
- Base amount = Fixed-base percentage × Average gross receipts
- Complex calculation requiring historical data
- Generally better for established companies

### Alternative Simplified Credit (ASC)
- 14% of QREs exceeding 50% of average QREs from prior 3 years
- Simpler calculation
- Better for companies with limited historical data
- More commonly used

### Basic Research Credit
- 20% of amounts paid to qualified organizations for basic research
- Separate from regular credit
- Universities and research organizations

## Credit Rates Summary

| Method | Rate | Base |
|--------|------|------|
| Regular Credit | 20% | QREs over base amount |
| Alternative Simplified | 14% | QREs over 50% of 3-year avg |
| Startup No History | 6% | All QREs |

## Requirements

### Four-Part Test (All Must Be Met)

**1. Permitted Purpose**
- Development of new or improved:
  - Product
  - Process
  - Technique
  - Formula
  - Invention
  - Software

**2. Technological Uncertainty**
- Uncertainty regarding:
  - Capability
  - Method
  - Design
  - Must exist at project outset

**3. Process of Experimentation**
- Systematic trial and error
- Modeling or simulation
- Evaluation of alternatives
- Scientific method applied

**4. Technological in Nature**
- Relies on principles of:
  - Physical sciences
  - Biological sciences
  - Engineering
  - Computer science

### Qualified Research Expenses (QREs)

| Expense Type | Qualification |
|--------------|--------------|
| Employee wages | 100% for qualified activities |
| Supplies | 100% if consumed in research |
| Contract research | 65% of payments (80% for small business) |
| Computer lease | Limited circumstances |

### Excluded Activities
- Research after commercial production begins
- Adaptation of existing products for specific customer
- Duplication of existing products
- Surveys and studies
- Research funded by another party
- Foreign research
- Social sciences research

## Implementation Steps

### Step 1: Activity Assessment

1. **Identify Potential Activities**
   - Interview technical staff
   - Review project documentation
   - Identify failed and successful projects
   - Document technical challenges

2. **Apply Four-Part Test**
   - Confirm permitted purpose
   - Document technological uncertainty
   - Evidence process of experimentation
   - Verify technological nature

### Step 2: QRE Calculation

1. **Wage Analysis**
   - Identify employees involved in R&D
   - Determine percentage of time in qualified activities
   - Calculate qualified wages
   - Include employer payroll taxes

2. **Supply Expenses**
   - Identify supplies used in research
   - Must be consumed or destroyed
   - Exclude general and administrative supplies
   - Calculate qualified amount

3. **Contract Research**
   - Identify third-party research payments
   - Apply 65% or 80% factor
   - Exclude routine services
   - Verify research elements

### Step 3: Credit Calculation

1. **Select Method**
   - Evaluate RRC vs. ASC
   - Consider historical data availability
   - Model both methods
   - Choose most beneficial

2. **Calculate Credit**
   - Apply appropriate formula
   - Deduct Section 280C reduction (if applicable)
   - Determine final credit amount

### Step 4: Documentation

1. **Technical Documentation**
   - Project descriptions
   - Uncertainty identified
   - Experiments conducted
   - Results achieved

2. **Financial Documentation**
   - Payroll records
   - Time tracking
   - Supply invoices
   - Contract agreements

3. **Contemporaneous Records**
   - Best if created during research
   - IRS scrutinizes after-the-fact documentation
   - Employee interviews help but less reliable

### Step 5: Filing

1. **Form 6765**
   - Report qualifying expenses
   - Calculate credit amount
   - Elect credit method

2. **State Returns**
   - Many states have R&D credits
   - Different rules may apply
   - File required state forms

## Payroll Tax Offset (Startups)

### Qualified Small Business Election
- Gross receipts less than $5 million
- No gross receipts more than 5 years prior
- Up to $500,000 credit against payroll taxes
- Elect on Form 6765

### Example
**Tech Startup Year 3:**
- No income tax liability (losses)
- R&D credit calculated: $150,000
- Payroll taxes due: $75,000/quarter
- **Payroll tax offset**: $75,000/quarter (up to $500,000 total)

## Risks & Considerations

### Audit Risk
- R&D credits are audit priority
- Must substantiate four-part test
- Documentation critical
- Consider contemporaneous records

### Common Audit Issues
- Activities don't meet four-part test
- Wages not properly allocated
- Inadequate documentation
- Software not "new or improved"
- Production activities included

### 280C Election
- Credit reduces R&D expense deduction
- Can elect reduced credit instead
- Net effective rate approximately 13%
- Analysis required for optimal choice

### State Conformity
- Most states have R&D credits
- Rules vary significantly
- Some states more generous
- File separate state claims

## Case Study

### Scenario
Manufacturing company with $50 million revenue, 15 engineers developing improved products and processes.

### Activity Identification

| Project | Description | QRE Wages |
|---------|-------------|----------:|
| Product A redesign | Improved durability | $180,000 |
| New production process | Automation development | $220,000 |
| Software integration | Custom ERP modules | $150,000 |
| Material testing | Alternative components | $80,000 |
| **Total** | | **$630,000** |

**Additional QREs:**
- Supplies: $45,000
- Contract research: $100,000 × 65% = $65,000

**Total QREs: $740,000**

### Credit Calculation (ASC Method)

**Historical QREs:**
- Year 1: $600,000
- Year 2: $650,000
- Year 3: $680,000
- 3-year average: $643,333

**Credit Calculation:**
- Current year QREs: $740,000
- 50% of average: $321,667
- Excess QREs: $418,333
- Credit rate: 14%
- **Gross credit: $58,567**

**After 280C reduction (election not made):**
- Reduced deduction savings foregone: ~$14,000
- **Net benefit: ~$44,000**

## Effective Dates

| Provision | Effective Date | Status |
|-----------|---------------|--------|
| R&D Tax Credit | Economic Recovery Tax Act 1981 | Made permanent December 18, 2015 (PATH Act) |
| Alternative Simplified Credit | 2007 | Permanent |
| Payroll Tax Offset (Startups) | January 1, 2016 (PATH Act) | Permanent - Up to $500,000 annually |
| Section 174 Capitalization | January 1, 2022 | Active - 5-year amortization domestic / 15-year foreign |

**2026 Tax Year Considerations**:
- R&D tax credit remains fully available and is now a permanent part of the tax code.
- **Important**: Section 174 requires capitalization and amortization of R&D expenses (5 years domestic, 15 years foreign) starting in 2022. This reduces the immediate deduction but credit remains available.
- Startup payroll tax offset of up to $500,000 continues for qualified small businesses.
- State R&D credits vary; check state conformity.
