# Captive Insurance

## Overview

A captive insurance company is a licensed insurance company owned by a business (or group of businesses) that insures the risks of its owner(s). Captive insurance allows businesses to deduct insurance premiums for risks that may be uninsurable or expensive in the commercial market, while building a reserve fund that may be accessed in the future.

## Key Benefits

- Tax deduction for insurance premiums paid
- Coverage for risks not available commercially
- Potential for underwriting profits
- Investment income within captive
- Asset protection benefits
- Risk management discipline

## Small Insurance Company Election (831(b))

### Overview
- Premium income up to $2.65 million (2024) can elect 831(b) status
- Only investment income taxed (not underwriting profits)
- Creates significant tax deferral opportunity
- Popular structure for closely-held businesses

### Key Features
| Feature | 831(b) Captive | Regular Captive |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| Max Premiums | $2.65M (2024) | Unlimited |
| Taxed On | Investment income only | Underwriting + investment |
| Complexity | Moderate | Higher |
| IRS Scrutiny | High | Moderate |

## Requirements

### Captive Formation Requirements
- Licensed insurance company structure
- Domiciled in approved jurisdiction
- Adequate capitalization (typically $250,000+)
- Professional management required

### Insurance Validity Requirements
- **Risk Distribution**: Cover multiple unrelated risks
- **Risk Shifting**: Transfer risk to different entity
- **Commonly Controlled Entities**: Need brother-sister arrangements
- **Arm's Length Premiums**: Actuarially determined

### 831(b) Specific Requirements
- Annual premiums not exceeding $2.65 million
- Diversification requirement (no more than 20% from any one policyholder)
- Related party limitation rules
- Must be properly licensed insurance company

## Client Fit Criteria

### Ideal Candidates
- Businesses paying $100,000+ in commercial insurance
- Companies with uninsured or underinsured risks
- Profitable businesses with consistent income
- Multiple related entities or high-risk operations
- Professional practices (medical, legal, accounting)
- Construction and real estate businesses

### Specific Risk Profiles
- Professional liability gaps
- Employment practices liability
- Cyber liability
- Supply chain interruption
- Regulatory defense costs
- Key person coverage gaps
- Terrorism/pandemic exclusions

### Poor Fit
- Small businesses with minimal risk exposure
- Businesses without genuine insurance needs
- Companies seeking only tax deduction
- Unstable or unprofitable businesses
- Those unwilling to commit to proper structure

## Implementation Steps

### Phase 1: Feasibility Study

1. **Risk Assessment**
   - Identify all business risks
   - Review current insurance coverage
   - Identify gaps and uninsured exposures
   - Quantify potential loss amounts

2. **Economic Analysis**
   - Calculate current insurance costs
   - Model captive premium payments
   - Project tax benefits
   - Analyze setup and ongoing costs

3. **Structure Evaluation**
   - Single-parent vs. group captive
   - 831(b) election viability
   - Domicile selection
   - Management structure

### Phase 2: Captive Formation

1. **Select Domicile**
   - Domestic: Vermont, Delaware, Utah, Nevada
   - Offshore: Bermuda, Cayman Islands, Isle of Man
   - Consider regulatory environment, costs, reputation

2. **Engage Professionals**
   - Captive manager
   - Actuary
   - Captive attorney
   - Tax advisor
   - Auditor

3. **Formation Documents**
   - Articles of incorporation
   - Bylaws
   - Business plan
   - Actuarial study
   - License application

4. **Capitalization**
   - Initial capital contribution (typically $250,000-$500,000)
   - Letter of credit (if permitted)
   - Parental guarantee (if required)

### Phase 3: Licensing and Operations

1. **Regulatory Approval**
   - Submit license application
   - Provide required documentation
   - Pay licensing fees
   - Obtain insurance license

2. **Operational Setup**
   - Open bank/investment accounts
   - Establish accounting systems
   - Create claims handling procedures
   - Set investment policy

3. **Policy Issuance**
   - Draft insurance policies
   - Actuarial premium determination
   - Policy execution
   - Premium payment

### Phase 4: Ongoing Compliance

1. **Annual Requirements**
   - Annual report to domicile regulator
   - Audited financial statements
   - Actuarial opinion
   - Form 1120-PC tax return
   - Board meetings (quarterly recommended)

2. **Claims Administration**
   - Proper claims reporting
   - Claims investigation
   - Reserve setting
   - Claims payment or denial

3. **Premium Validation**
   - Annual actuarial review
   - Premium adjustment as needed
   - Risk reassessment
   - Documentation updates

## Risks & Considerations

### IRS Scrutiny
- 831(b) captives are audit priority
- Economic substance required
- Actuarial support essential
- Documentation critical

### Aviva and Micro-Captive Cases
- Multiple Tax Court cases against abusive structures
- "Listed transaction" for certain arrangements
- Penalties for reportable transactions
- Heightened disclosure requirements

### Red Flags That Attract Audit
- Premium-to-policy ratio analysis
- Lack of legitimate risk transfer
- No claims ever paid
- Circular cash flows
- Unreasonable premium amounts

### Compliance Costs
- Annual actuarial fees ($15,000-$30,000)
- Captive management ($20,000-$40,000)
- Audit fees ($10,000-$20,000)
- Legal and tax compliance
- Total ongoing: $50,000-$100,000+ annually

### Exit Complexity
- Surplus extraction taxed
- Run-off period for claims
- Regulatory approval needed
- Liquidation complexity

## Case Study

### Scenario
ABC Construction, LLC generates $10 million annual revenue. Current commercial insurance: $250,000/year. Identified uninsured risks: $800,000 in potential exposures.

### Captive Structure

**Formation**
- 831(b) captive domiciled in Delaware
- Initial capitalization: $300,000
- Annual premiums: $400,000

**Coverage Provided**
| Risk | Annual Premium |
|------|---------------:|
| Construction defect | $120,000 |
| Warranty obligations | $80,000 |
| Subcontractor default | $75,000 |
| Equipment breakdown | $50,000 |
| Environmental cleanup | $45,000 |
| Key person | $30,000 |
| **Total** | **$400,000** |

### Tax Analysis

**Year 1**
- Premium deduction to operating company: $400,000
- Tax savings (37% + 5% state): $168,000
- Captive investment income tax: ~$5,000
- Net tax benefit: **$163,000**

**Less: Ongoing Costs**
- Captive management: $25,000
- Actuarial: $20,000
- Audit: $12,000
- Legal/regulatory: $8,000
- Total costs: $65,000

**Net Annual Benefit**: ~$98,000

**5-Year Projection**
- Cumulative premiums paid: $2,000,000
- Cumulative tax savings: ~$490,000
- If no major claims, surplus accumulates
- Potential distribution as dividends at lower rates
